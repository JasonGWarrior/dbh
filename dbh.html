The future of humanity and androids

Wildlife Androids

In 2038, androids serve humanity in nearly every role. They cook, clean, and assist humans in daily life. But not all androids are human-like. Some are designed to simulate other living creatures we once knew and loved.

Android Zoos

Thanks to CyberLife's advanced technology, you and your family can experience once-extinct animals in android form. Zoos around the world, opened by CyberLife, allow visitors to see not only animals we learned about in school, but also creatures dating back to prehistoric times.

But how does it all work?

The Intelligence Behind Them

The androids we know and rely on are trained using hundreds of petabytes of data from around the world: news articles, books, human conversations, videos, and many other sources.

Android animals, however, are different. They were trained using an estimated 40 terabytes of video data containing real wildlife behavior—mothers feeding their cubs, animals playing, and how groups interact in nature.

These androids are classified as "Filtered Animal Androids" (FAA). Their training data removes hunting scenes or violent attacks to prevent aggressive behavior and ensure the safety of visitors.

CyberLife "Personal Zoo"

You've heard the rumors—and yes, they are true. CyberLife owns a gated portion of the Amazon Rainforest used for advanced android research. Within this environment, CyberLife scientists observe a different class of android wildlife.

These are called "Unfiltered Animal Androids." Unlike the zoo models, their training data includes the full spectrum of natural behavior: hunting, survival conflicts, injury, and death.

These experimental models also use a system known as digital olfaction. Instead of relying only on cameras and audio sensors, the android animals are equipped with chemical detection sensors that simulate a sense of smell. This allows them to track scents in the environment, locate other animals, identify food sources, and follow scent trails—just as real wildlife would.

Because of this expanded dataset and sensory system, the android animals behave far more realistically—and far more dangerously. They are never shown to the public and remain strictly within CyberLife research zones.

The results of these experiments were both surprising and expected: the android animals behaved almost exactly like real wildlife.
